Root Causes

Statement:

The continual rising cost of fresh produce and lack of accesibility in primarily urban communities of color is a significant cause of food insecurity because it makes unhealthy processed foods the best alternative for urban families.

In the last decade, Oakland's urban parts have increased their population by over 50,000 people but construction of housing and markets can't keep up. (CDC Article)
"58% of the low-income rural group had access to garden produce while only 23% of the low-income urban group reported access." (Oakland North Website Article)
Food insecurity rates are at 8.4% in Alameda county. Food insecurity rates in the entire US is about 10.2%

Implications

Our city and state governments need to focus less on how many people we can fit into a small area and more on how we can make the living situations better for the people that are already residents.

Historical Context

Urban communities of color have long been neglected with their needs and wants often looked over, allowing food insecurity and inequality to rise.

The growing rate of Oakland's urban communities have allowed the city to focus on housing rather than offering fresh and healthy food and options to those who already live there
In Oakland, places with primarily African American and other minority households are more commonly considered food deserts
"People of color rarely qualified for FHA loans because these were to be applied only to newly constructed homes, and contrary to Howard’s vision of universalist garden cities that welcomed and nourished all workers, new home developments in the suburban industrial gardens were racially exclusive." (Essay by Nathan McClintock)

Implications

We need to allow the communities that are actually facing the problem to give their input and ideas on how to fix it. Only having people who have no idea what it’s actually like living in their situation helping can create more problems because they don’t know how to correctly address their issues at hand.
